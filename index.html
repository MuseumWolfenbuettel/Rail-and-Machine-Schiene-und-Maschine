7.Rail and Machine – Schiene und Maschine

“The colour and shape of life changed,” wrote Heinrich Heine. In the 19th century, people even described the profound change in their living and working conditions as a revolution – an industrial revolution. Economic and social circumstances were permanently transformed; an agrarian society became an industrial one. Not only did acceleration, progress, and productivity become determining factors in this new era; so, too, did alienation. In Wolfenbüttel, industrialisation was mainly a process that was decisively shaped by agriculture. With the beginning of the triumphal rise of the sugar beet in the mid-19th century and the great success of Wolfenbüttel vegetable growers as suppliers to the Brunswick canning industry, a time of growing prosperity in the town and country set in. After an economic depression that had lasted nearly 100 years, due to the removal of the court to Brunswick in the 18th century, things began to look up. The most perceptible sign of the new times was the tram line that connected Wolfenbüttel to Brunswick from 1897 to 1954.
